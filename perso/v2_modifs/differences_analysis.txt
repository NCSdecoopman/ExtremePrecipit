  ADDED:   The choice of the hydrological year instead of the calendar year is standard in French climatology and hydrology of extremes. In France, the most intense extreme precipitation events, particularly the Mediterranean episodes (e.g., Cévenol events), occur during autumn (September to November). Using a standard calendar year (January to December) would risk splitting a single autumn convective season across two different years, or grouping two distinct autumn seasons into the same year. This could violate the block-independence assumption required for the GEV modeling of annual maxima. Starting the hydrological year on September 1 ensures that each autumn convective season is kept fully intact within a single annual block.

The rationale for using this hierarchical approach, rather than simply selecting the model with the absolute smallest $p$-value across all tests, is to account for the differing degrees of freedom. Models $M_3$ and $M_3^*$ (where both location $\mu$ and scale $\sigma$ vary over time) have 2 degrees of freedom relative to $M_0$, whereas models $M_1/M_1^*$ and $M_2/M_2^*$ have only 1 degree of freedom. Choosing the model with the absolute smallest $p$-value without considering these differences would be statistically inconsistent and could bias the selection toward simpler models or lead to overfitting. Our two-step procedure first checks if a simultaneous change in both parameters ($M_3$ or $M_3^*$) is statistically justified. If it is not, it then evaluates whether a simpler change in only one parameter ($M_1/M_1^*$ or $M_2/M_2^*$) is justified. This hierarchical selection structure prevents overfitting and ensures that complex multi-parameter trends are selected only when there is strong statistical evidence for them.
